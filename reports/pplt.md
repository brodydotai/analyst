## Research

### 1. Supply Architecture & Production Constraints

Global platinum mine production is approximately 5.5-6.0M oz annually, with South Africa contributing over 70% — primarily from the Bushveld Complex operated by Anglo American Platinum (Amplats), Impala Platinum (Implats), and Sibanye-Stillwater. This extreme geographic concentration creates structural supply risk. South African production faces a triple constraint: Eskom electricity shortages causing intermittent load-shedding that disrupts mining operations, aging ore bodies with declining head grades requiring deeper and more expensive extraction, and rising labor costs as miners demand inflation-adjusted wages. Russian production (~11% of global supply from Nornickel) faces sanctions uncertainty. Recycling (secondary supply) has been declining as autocatalyst scrap volumes decrease — fewer diesel vehicles are being scrapped, and the shift to gasoline (palladium-dominant) and EVs (no PGM catalyst) reduces the platinum recycling pipeline. The WPIC estimates the 2025 deficit at ~850,000 oz, and projects persistent deficits through 2029, narrowing progressively to ~486,000 oz as recycling eventually recovers. New mine development is virtually nonexistent at current economics. ([Source: WPIC Platinum Essentials](https://platinuminvestment.com/investment-research/essentials))

### 2. Demand Decomposition & Hydrogen Economy

Platinum demand is approximately 7.5-8.0M oz annually, split across automotive autocatalysts (~35-40%), jewelry (~25-30%), industrial applications (~20-25%), and investment (~5-10%). The automotive segment has been under pressure as diesel market share declines in Europe, but this is being offset by rising demand from emissions standards tightening in China and India, where diesel adoption is growing. The transformative demand vector is the hydrogen economy: platinum is the optimal catalyst for PEM (Proton Exchange Membrane) electrolyzers used in green hydrogen production and for fuel cell electric vehicles. The WPIC forecasts hydrogen-related platinum demand to grow by 875,000-900,000 oz by 2030 — equivalent to adding ~12-13% to total current demand. Heavy-duty transport (long-haul trucks, buses, trains) is the primary FCEV market, where battery weight and charging time limitations make fuel cells the preferred zero-emission solution. Japan, South Korea, and the EU are leading hydrogen infrastructure deployment, with significant government funding commitments. This emerging demand sector is the primary structural catalyst for platinum's re-rating. ([Source: CME Group Platinum](https://www.cmegroup.com/articles/2025/whats-next-for-platinum-investment.html))

### 3. Macro Regime & Precious Metals Rotation

Platinum's 2025-2026 surge has been amplified by a precious metals rotation theme. After gold's strong run to ~$2,900/oz, institutional investors are rotating into cheaper precious metals — the platinum-to-gold ratio of ~0.60x is near historic lows (the long-term average is 0.8-1.0x, and platinum historically traded at a premium to gold). This ratio compression reflects a decade of platinum underperformance post-2015 diesel emissions scandal (Dieselgate), which cratered diesel vehicle demand and platinum autocatalyst consumption. The re-rating thesis argues that hydrogen economy demand replaces lost diesel demand, while supply constraints prevent production response. Central bank monetary easing (Fed rate cuts expected through 2026) provides a macro tailwind for all precious metals, but platinum's industrial demand growth adds a cyclical upside component that gold lacks. The risk is that platinum's industrial beta makes it vulnerable in a recession scenario where both auto and industrial demand contract simultaneously. ([Source: FXEmpire Platinum Forecast](https://www.fxempire.com/forecasts/article/platinum-price-forecast-gold-rotation-fuels-platinum-breakout-toward-2300-by-2026-1567402))

### 4. PPLT Fund Structure & Investment Mechanics

PPLT holds allocated platinum bars in a JPMorgan London vault, with each share backed by approximately 1/10th of an ounce. The 0.60% expense ratio is competitive for physical precious metals ETFs (GLD charges 0.40%, SLV 0.50%). The fund does not pay dividends — returns are entirely from platinum spot price appreciation. AUM of $2.06B reflects significant asset growth from both price appreciation and inflows. As a physically-backed ETF, PPLT tracks spot platinum with minimal tracking error and no futures roll cost. The key structural advantage over mining equities is simplicity: no operational risk, no dilution, no management execution concerns. The disadvantage is no leverage — miners typically provide 2-3x spot price leverage on the upside (and downside). For investors seeking pure platinum commodity exposure without mining complexity, PPLT is the most direct U.S.-listed vehicle. The 109% 12-month return demonstrates that even unleveraged physical exposure has delivered exceptional returns in this supply-constrained market. ([Source: ETF.com PPLT](https://www.etf.com/PPLT))
