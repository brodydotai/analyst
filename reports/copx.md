## Research

### 1. Operator Profile & Asset Quality Classification

COPX tracks the Solactive Global Copper Miners Total Return Index with 40 constituents across the global copper mining universe. The portfolio is diversified across major, mid-tier, and junior copper producers.

**Top 10 Holdings (50.4% of portfolio):** KGHM Polska Miedz (5.78%), Lundin Mining (5.52%), Boliden AB (5.41%), Southern Copper (5.25%), Freeport-McMoRan (4.93%), Hudbay Minerals (4.90%), Sumitomo Metal Mining (4.87%), Glencore (4.80%), Antofagasta (4.67%), Teck Resources (4.30%).

The geographic footprint spans Chile (largest exposure via Antofagasta, KGHM Sierra Gorda, Glencore, Lundin), Peru (Southern Copper, Hudbay Constancia), Indonesia (FCX Grasberg), Poland (KGHM domestic), Canada (Teck, Hudbay, Lundin HQ), and the Nordic region (Boliden). This creates significant South American concentration — roughly 40%+ of production exposure sits in Chile/Peru, both facing labor unrest and permitting challenges.

### 2. Mine-Level Economics

**Freeport-McMoRan (4.93% weight):** US operations AISC at $3.03/lb in 2025, targeting $2.50/lb for 2026 through efficiency improvements. Grasberg (Indonesia) is a world-class low-cost operation providing cost-curve advantage.

**Southern Copper (5.25% weight):** Industry-leading cash cost of $0.42/lb in Q3 2025. 2026 production guidance of 911,400 tonnes. Operations concentrated in Peru and Mexico with some Ecuador exposure.

**Industry benchmarks:** Global average AISC approximately $2.52/lb ($5,565/mt). Kamoa-Kakula (DRC) at $2.30/lb represents the lower quartile. Capital intensity for new projects has doubled to $15,000-20,000/tonne capacity, creating a structural barrier to rapid supply response.

**Ore grade crisis:** Global average copper grade has declined from 1.2% to 0.6% over the past 25 years, requiring ever-increasing tonnage to maintain production levels. This is the single most important structural constraint on supply growth — it raises costs, extends timelines, and limits the pace at which new projects can compensate for depletion.

### 3. Copper Price Macro Framework

**Current pricing:** LME copper at $13,250/mt (~$6.01/lb) as of late February 2026, trading near record highs. The YTD 2026 range has been $8,977-$9,995/mt on an average basis with significant intra-period volatility.

**Forecasts:** Goldman Sachs raised its year-end 2026 target to $12,200/mt (March 2026 update), with a full-year average of $11,400/mt. JP Morgan sees Q2 2026 peaking at ~$12,500/mt with a full-year average of ~$12,075/mt. The bull case ($12,500-$13,500/mt) requires supply deficit acceleration and AI demand exceeding expectations. The bear case ($10,000-$10,750/mt) hinges on China demand slowdown or tariff shock.

**Supply-demand balance:** JP Morgan projects a 330,000-tonne refined copper deficit in 2026 — the largest in years. Mine supply growth is estimated at only 1.4% (~500,000 tonnes) while refined production growth collapses to 0.9% as concentrate availability becomes the binding constraint. However, 2025 actually ran a 600,000-tonne surplus, and some forecasters see continued excess supply from rising scrap availability.

**Structural demand drivers:** AI data center grid infrastructure projected to consume 1.1 million metric tonnes annually by 2030. EVs require 2.9x more copper than conventional vehicles. Global copper demand forecast to grow from 28M tonnes (2025) to 42M tonnes (2040) — a 50% increase driven by electrification, renewables, and digital infrastructure.

### 5. Pattern Matching & IDPs

**Chile 5-month production decline:** Chile's copper output declined for 5 consecutive months in late 2025 — a concerning pattern from the world's largest producer (23% of global supply). Combined with Peru's 12% YoY production loss from social unrest, South American supply disruptions are a persistent headwind.

**Concentrate bottleneck:** The critical IDP in 2026 is that refined production growth (0.9%) is collapsing below mine supply growth (1.4%) because smelters face concentrate scarcity. This bottleneck amplifies the deficit signal — even if mines produce more ore, refined copper availability is constrained.

**Tariff distortion pattern:** The existing 50% semi-finished copper tariff and pending 15% refined tariff are creating pricing distortions between US and global markets. This could artificially inflate US-facing copper prices while depressing prices elsewhere, creating basis risk for ETF investors.

**Momentum exhaustion risk:** The 4x AUM growth in 6 months and $2B single-month inflow are hallmarks of late-cycle momentum positioning. Historically, such concentrated inflows into commodity ETFs precede volatility spikes as positions become crowded.

**China PMI stall pattern:** Manufacturing PMI at 50.1 (barely expansionary) with business sentiment at 9-month low mirrors the 2014-2015 China slowdown pattern. If the 15th Five-Year Plan pivot to advanced manufacturing doesn't offset property drag, copper demand growth could disappoint.
