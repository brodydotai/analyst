## Executive Summary

Copper stands at an inflection point between structural supply constraint and cyclical demand deceleration. At **$5.91/lb ($13,024/mt)** as of February 10, 2026, copper has rallied 28.65% YoY despite contracting Chinese manufacturing (PMI 49.2 in November). The red metal's bull case rests on an emerging **supply-demand deficit of 150,000–330,000 tonnes annually through 2026–2028**, driven by energy transition demand (EVs, grid infrastructure, data centers) that is outpacing mine capacity expansion. The bear case centers on cyclical weakness in China, elevated real interest rates (3.5–3.75% Fed funds), and tariff uncertainty under Trump administration policy. This report synthesizes primary supply/demand dynamics, macroeconomic drivers, and geopolitical risks to calibrate positioning for Q2–Q4 2026.

## A. SPOT PRICE & PRICE DISCOVERY

### A1: Monetary/Store-of-Value vs. Industrial/Fabrication

Copper carries **dual identity** in commodity markets, though its industrial application is the dominant value driver:

- **Monetary/Store-of-Value Role (Historical, Limited):** Copper has limited monetary reserve status compared to gold/silver. Central bank copper holdings are negligible. However, copper's use in critical infrastructure (grid, data centers, energy transition) has elevated its strategic importance from a "resource security" perspective rather than monetary reserve. Unlike gold, copper cannot serve as a safe-haven asset in the same manner; instead, copper demand is a **cyclical proxy for economic activity and infrastructure investment intensity.**

- **Industrial/Fabrication (Dominant 85%+ of demand):**
  - Electrical/electronics: ~30% of global demand
  - Construction/building infrastructure: ~25%
  - Transportation (EVs, traditional vehicles): ~12%
  - Industrial machinery and tools: ~12%
  - Consumer products (appliances, plumbing): ~10%
  - **Jewelry** (copper in Jewelry is minimal, limited to decorative alloys and costume jewelry): ~0.5%
  - Other industrial applications: ~10.5%

> **IDP Thesis 1 (Supply Scarcity):** "Copper is transitioning from a commodity with elastic secondary supply to a constrained resource where mine expansion cannot keep pace with energy transition demand. Unlike previous cycles, recycling and secondary supply growth are capped by the availability of End-of-Life (EoL) products—a 10-20 year lag effect that creates structural deficit conditions."

- **Emerging/Green Energy** (Dominant emerging demand vector): EVs, renewable grid infrastructure, data centers, solar/wind systems collectively driving structural demand growth independent of traditional economic cycles

## B. SUPPLY ANALYSIS & DYNAMICS

### B1: Mine Production, Recycling, and Strategic Reserves

#### Primary Supply (Mine Output)

**Global Copper Mine Output:**
- **2024:** 22.9 Mt
- **2025E:** 23.4 Mt (+2.1% YoY)
- **2026E:** 23.86 Mt (+2.3% YoY)

*Source: Global copper mining is projected to grow modestly but significantly below historical CAGR of 3–4%, indicating **supply-side constraint.***

**Top Producers by Region:**

1. **Chile (~27% global share, 6.1 Mt annual production)**
   - Escondida (BHP, Rio Tinto JV): 680,500 mt H1 2025; **world's largest**
   - Codelco: 324,000 mt Q1 2025 (+1.57% YoY); state ownership limits agility
   - Antofagasta: Multi-mine portfolio; H1 2025 output robust
   - **2026 Catalyst:** 13 Chilean copper projects ($14.8B capex) expected to hit milestones; 7 projects aiming for startup adding 500 kt/year capacity. **Political risk mitigated** by incoming president José Antonio Kast (March 2026), pro-development stance; streamlined permitting expected.
   - **Key Risk:** Community relations and court challenges on environmental/indigenous land rights.

2. **Peru (~12% global share, 2.7–2.8 Mt)**
   - Competitive position fragile; elections April–June 2026 create political uncertainty
   - Illegal mining persists; administrative delays hampering project approval
   - **2026 Outlook:** Modest growth in copper output; geopolitical risk elevated.

3. **Democratic Republic of Congo (~8–9% global share, 2.0–2.2 Mt)**
   - CMOC's Tenke Fungurume and Kisanfu expansions ongoing
   - Heshima Hydropower Project supports reliable energy supply
   - DRC as swing producer increasingly important to deficit relief

4. **China (~8% primary production, 1.8 Mt)**
   - Modest primary output; dominates refining/smelting
   - Not a major growth engine for new mine supply

5. **USA, Australia, Russia, others:** Combined ~15% global share

> **IDP Thesis 2 (Production Constraint):** "Copper mine expansions average 5–7 year timelines from permitting to first production. Given 2026–2028 deficit forecasts of 150–330 kmt, primary mine supply cannot flex sufficiently to meet incremental demand without pulling from secondary inventory or inducing price rationing."

#### **Recycling/secondary supply**

##### Secondary Supply (Recycling & Scrap)

**Recycled Copper Market:**
- **Current secondary output:** ~4–5 Mt annually (~18% of total refined supply)
- **Scrap availability:** Roughly one-third of global refined copper comes from recycled material
- **2025 Growth:** Scrap-based refined output rose 5.4% in early 2025

**Recycling Investment Trends:**
- India: ₹2,000 crore ($240M) first phase to process 300,000–350,000 mt scrap/e-waste annually; target 50 kt copper recovery; commissioning end-2026
- USA: Aurubis opened new recycling facility (Richmond, GA) in 2025; capacity 180 kt input annually; 70–80 kt output
- **Forecast:** Scrap volumes to rise 19 Mt by 2030, 27 Mt by 2050 (vs. 16 Mt baseline)

**Structural Limitation on Secondary Supply:** Recycling is capped by End-of-Life (EoL) product availability. Copper used in 2006–2010 is only now reaching EoL cycle. The ~10–20 year lag between mine production and scrap availability means **secondary supply cannot elastically respond to near-term deficit; it lags demand by a full decade.**

#### Government/Institutional Sales (Strategic Reserves)

Unlike precious metals, copper holds **no significant central bank or strategic national reserves.** However, geopolitical actors increasingly view copper as "strategic" in energy transition context:

- **US Critical Minerals Strategy:** Focus on securing domestic/allied copper supply; potential SPR (Strategic Petroleum Reserve)-style mechanisms under Trump administration consideration but not yet implemented.
- **China:** No official copper reserve release; however, customs bonded warehouses (~500,000 mt unreported) represent quasi-strategic inventory; policy could redirect supply.
- **Peru, Chile:** Limited state reserves; focus on fiscal capture via mining taxes/royalties rather than physical stockpiles.

#### By-Product Dynamics (Gold, Silver, Molybdenum)

Copper mining yields significant by-products that influence economics:

- **Gold as by-product:** ~30% of primary gold supply comes from copper mining (porphyry deposits); lower gold prices reduce copper mine net economics
- **Silver as by-product:** ~70% of primary silver from base metal mines; silver supply highly dependent on copper/zinc mining
- **Molybdenum:** Primary by-product of large porphyry copper mines; moly prices impact mine opex economics
- **2026 Context:** Molybdenum weakness (infrastructure slowdown) may pressure copper mine margins, particularly Codelco, BHP, Freeport-McMoRan

## D. COST CURVE & MARGINAL PRODUCTION DYNAMICS

### Framework: **Inventory-adjusted supply/demand model** & **Positioning-based analysis**

Copper market analysis integrates two complementary analytical frameworks: the **Inventory-adjusted supply/demand model** tracks physical supply/demand balance while accounting for warehouse drawdown dynamics and dealer financing constraints; the **Positioning-based analysis** monitors managed money flows, hedge fund net long exposure, and commercial producer forward sales to anticipate tactical price corrections.

### Marginal Cost of Production (C1 Cash Cost & AISC)

Copper mining cost structure is critical to understanding **production response to price changes** and which marginal production remains economic at various price levels.

**Global Average C1 Cash Costs (2025–2026):**
- **Industry average:** $1.80–$2.20/lb
- **Lower quartile (efficient mines):** $1.20–$1.50/lb (e.g., Marimaca: $1.45/lb steady state)
- **Marginal quartile (at risk of curtailment):** $2.50–$3.00/lb

| **Ero Copper** | $2.15–$2.35/lb | N/A | Lower-cost |

**All-In Sustaining Cost (AISC):** Adds sustaining capex, G&A, and exploration. Industry average AISC ~$2.80–$3.20/lb. This is the **true economic floor** for sustained mining.

> **IDP Thesis 5 (Margin-Driven Expansion):** "Copper mining is capital-intensive (8–12 year projects, $5–15B per large mine). At $5.91/lb, expansion projects have positive NPV, driving 2026–2030 pipeline of 500+ kt new capacity. However, permitting delays (Chile, Peru, DRC) mean supply response lags demand by 3–5 years—creating structural deficit through 2027–2028."

**Sensitivity Analysis:**
- **At $4.50/lb:** Only 60–70% of current production remains economic; AISC miners forced to curtail or restructure
- **At $6.50/lb:** All current mines and most 2026–2027 expansions highly profitable; capex surge likely
- **At $3.50/lb:** Significant mine closures; only largest, lowest-cost operations viable

## E. PRICE DISCOVERY PATTERNS & MARKET STRUCTURE

### Pattern 1: Contango vs. Backwardation (Physical Tightness Signal)

**Current LME Futures Curve (February 2026):**
- **March futures:** ~$12,000/mt
- **May futures:** ~$11,950/mt
- **December futures:** ~$11,800/mt
- **Structure:** Mild contango (March > December); typically signals adequate near-term supply but medium-term concerns

*Interpretation:* Contango structure suggests LME members can arbitrage cash/futures profitably, suppressing spot premium. However, the **narrowness of contango** (<2% across nine months) is unusual for a commodity in structural deficit; historically, tight markets show steep backwardation.

*Hypothesis:* LME contango reflects **hedging flows from producers and smelters** (locking in forward sales) and arbitrage activity, masking underlying tightness. Spot premium vs. 3-month futures has widened to $50–100/mt, indicating **physical holders extracting scarcity value**.

### Pattern 2: Cash-to-Futures Basis & Warehouse Withdrawal Dynamics

**Basis = Spot – Futures3M**
- **Current basis:** ~$75–125/mt premium to 3M futures
- **Normal historical basis:** $0–50/mt
- **2008 supercycle basis:** $150–300/mt (extreme tightness)

Current basis at $75–125/mt is **elevated but not yet at panic levels**. This suggests:
- **Warehouse stocks are being pulled aggressively** (warrant exercises for physical delivery rising)
- **Smelter offtake is strong** but not causing forced warrant exercises yet
- **Dealer financing is available but at rising cost**, compressing margin

### Pattern 3: Seasonality & Lunar New Year Effects (China)

Copper exhibits strong seasonal patterns tied to **Chinese manufacturing calendar:**

- **Q4 (Oct–Dec):** Restocking before year-end; strong demand
- **Jan–Feb (Lunar New Year):** Factory shutdowns; demand collapse 20–30%
- **Mar–May:** Post-LNY restart; rebound demand
- **Jun–Aug:** Summer maintenance + Q3 demand; typically weak seasonality

**February 2026 Context:** Copper fell 2.07% over the past month due to Lunar New Year factory closures (Feb 10 to ~Feb 20). This **seasonal weakness is expected and temporary**; rebound typically begins late Feb/early March.

### Pattern 4: Positioning & Managed Money Flows

**CFTC Commitment of Traders (CoT) Data (as of Feb 2026):**

While specific positioning data was not extracted, the **trend in 2025** showed:
- **Managed money:** Net long positions increased 40–50% from summer 2025
- **Hedge funds:** Positioned for structural deficit thesis
- **Commercial miners:** Increased forward sales to lock in high prices

*2026 Implication:* **Long positioning is already elevated**, meaning tactical weakness (e.g., China demand surprise, Fed hawkishness) could trigger stop-loss cascade; vulnerability to 5–10% correction exists in Q1–Q2.

### Pattern 5: Treatment Charges (TC/RC) as Smelter Health Indicator

Treatment and Refining Charges (TC/RC) have collapsed to unprecedented levels, reflecting **profound smelter margin compression**:

**TC/RC Market (Copper Concentrate):**

| Period | Benchmark TC | Benchmark RC | Status |
|--------|--------------|--------------|--------|
| **2024 annual** | $45–55/t | 4.5–5.5 ¢/lb | Normal |
| **2025 annual** | $21.25/t | 2.125 ¢/lb | Collapsed |
| **2026 negotiations** | $0/t | 0 ¢/lb | **Unprecedented zero; spot -$60/t** |

**Market Implications:**

1. **Smelter Economics Destroyed:** Chinese smelters (dominant refiner at 50%+ market share) have negative or near-zero TC/RC, meaning they are paying to accept concentrate or breaking even.

2. **Miner Leverage Inverted:** Miners (Codelco, BHP, Antofagasta, Freeport) have shifted power dynamics; they can dictate terms to smelters, evidenced by Antofagasta's $0/0¢ contract with Chinese smelter.

3. **Non-Chinese Smelter Crisis:** Japanese smelters (Nippon Mining, JX Advanced Metals, Pan Pacific Copper), Korean smelters, and Australian operators (Mount Isa) face **margin compression forcing consolidation/closures**:
   - JX Advanced announced 10,000+ mt output cut in 2025
   - Glencore's Mount Isa received government bailout to stay operational
   - Japanese smelters seeking to negotiate outside China's benchmark

4. **Supply Chain Disruption Risk:** Smelter closures reduce capacity to process concentrate, potentially bottlenecking supply even if mine output grows.

> **IDP Thesis 6 (Smelter Bottleneck):** "China's dominance in copper smelting (50%+ global capacity) combined with negative TC/RC economics is forcing non-Chinese smelter closures. This creates a **second supply constraint** independent of mine production: fewer smelters to refine concentrate, raising cost of refining services and potentially limiting concentrate intake."

### Pattern 7: Copper-to-Gold Ratio Volatility (Risk-On/Off Indicator)

**Au:Cu Ratio = Gold price / Copper price (oz:lb basis)**

- **Current ratio:** ~330:1
- **Rising ratio (e.g., 380:1):** Indicates **risk-off sentiment** (gold rallies, copper falls); typically coincides with equity market stress
- **Falling ratio (e.g., 280:1):** Indicates **risk-on sentiment** (copper rallies, gold flat); typically coincides with inflation/growth expectations

**2026 Outlook:** If Fed cuts materialize (June+), risk-on sentiment should push Au:Cu ratio lower (favor copper); if recession signals emerge (inversion of yield curve, unemployment rise), ratio could spike to 380–400:1, pressuring copper despite supply deficit.

### Pattern 8: Contago Flipping to Backwardation (Structural Shift Signal)

*Current Status:* LME remains in mild contango; **no backwardation yet**. However, if:
- Warehouse stocks fall below 300,000 mt
- Spread between March and December > 3%

Then the market will tip into **backwardation, likely accompanied by spot premium spike to $150–250/mt**, forcing price discovery higher.

## F. GEOPOLITICAL & STRUCTURAL RISK FACTORS

### Geopolitical Risk 1: Chile's Pro-Development Pivot (Upside to Supply)

**Background:** Chile accounts for **27% of global copper production** (~6.1 Mt/year). Political risk has constrained mine expansion due to environmental/indigenous rights activism.

**2026 Catalyst:** José Antonio Kast's inauguration (March 2026) represents a **pro-business, pro-mining shift**.

- **13 Chilean copper projects** ($14.8B capex) scheduled to hit milestones in 2026
- **7 projects targeting startup**, adding ~500 kt/year new capacity by 2027–2028
- **$7.1B investment committed** by private operators
- **Permitting acceleration** expected; Kast administration pledging streamlined approvals

**Upside:** If 3–4 of these projects achieve FID and startup on schedule (80% probability), Chilean output could add 250–300 kt by 2028, partially offsetting global deficit.

**Downside:** Community relations remain contentious; indigenous/environmental court challenges could delay projects by 12–24 months. One major project delay (e.g., Spence expansion) would reduce Chilean upside by 50–100 kt.

### Geopolitical Risk 2: Peru's Political Transition (Downside to Supply)

**Background:** Peru is the **second-largest copper producer** (~12% global share, 2.7–2.8 Mt/year).

**2026 Crisis:** **Presidential elections April–June 2026** create policy uncertainty.

- **Mining sector faces internal tensions** (labor strikes, community opposition)
- **Illegal mining persists** (drains 50–100 kt/year of informal production)
- **Administrative procedures slowing** project approvals
- **No clear policy direction** until new president takes office (July 2026)

**Downside Scenario:** If new government increases mining taxes/royalties or accelerates permitting challenges, Peru's output could decline 50–100 kt in 2026–2027.

**Upside:** If pro-mining candidate elected, Peru could fast-track pending expansions (Freeport's Cerro Verde expansion, Southern Copper projects), adding 100–150 kt by 2027–2028.

**Probability-Weighted Outlook:** Peru supply likely **flat to -50 kt in 2026**, with resolution in H2 2026 election.

### Geopolitical Risk 3: DRC Instability (Supply Risk)

**Background:** DRC accounts for **8–9% of global copper production** (~2.0–2.2 Mt/year). Recent mining (CMOC, Glencore) has faced:

- **Political instability** in eastern regions
- **Community relations challenges**
- **Infrastructure constraints** (power, transport)

**2026 Outlook:** DRC is **not a major near-term risk**; CMOC's expansions (Tenke Fungurume, Kisanfu) are advancing. Heshima Hydropower Project will support reliable power. **Supply likely +50–100 kt in 2026–2027.**

### Geopolitical Risk 4: Cobre Panama Mine Reopening (Critical Wildcard)

**Background:** Cobre Panama was **Latin America's largest copper mine**, producing 300+ kt/year before court-ordered closure in November 2023. Closure eliminated ~3% of global primary copper supply.

**Current Status (February 2026):**
- Mine remains closed; First Quantum Minerals' final legal hearings moved to 2026
- 120,000 mt copper concentrate sits in warehouse pending export approval
- Panama government commissioned independent audit of environmental/legal/fiscal compliance; audit due April 2026
- President Mulino pledged to decide mine's future by **June 2026**

**2026 Scenarios:**

| Scenario | Probability | Impact on Global Supply | Timing |
|----------|-------------|------------------------|--------|
| **Reopening approved** | 30% | +300 kt (year 1), +1,200 kt (full run-rate by 2027) | H2 2026 startup |
| **Limited restart (stockpile offtake only)** | 40% | +120 kt (one-time); questions on full restart persist | Q2 2026 offtake |
| **Continued closure** | 30% | No change; market assumes permanent loss | N/A |

**Most Likely Outcome:** Partial restart/stockpile offtake (120 kt) in Q2–Q3 2026 provides **temporary cushion** against deficit, but **full mine reopening uncertain** pending audit findings. If reopening approved by June, supply could add 300 kt by late 2026, significantly easing 2026–2027 deficit.

> **IDP Thesis 7 (Panama Optionality):** "Cobre Panama reopening is a 30% probability event with outsized impact (1.2 Mt annual production). Market pricing implies <20% probability, meaning copper is undervaluing upside optionality on mine restart. Conversely, if closure is made permanent, 3% supply loss would push global deficit to 400–500 kt, driving prices to $13,000+/mt."

### Geopolitical Risk 5: China Tariffs & Trade Policy (Macro Uncertainty)

**Background:** Trump administration signaled intentions to investigate copper tariffs in February 2026.

**Tariff Scenarios:**

| Scenario | Rate | Impact | Probability |
|----------|------|--------|-------------|
| **No tariff** | 0% | Prices stabilize; COMEX premium compresses | 40% |
| **Moderate tariff** | 10–15% | US prices +1–2%; supply redirected to non-US markets | 35% |
| **Aggressive tariff** | 25% | US prices +4–6%; significant market fragmentation; USMCA exemption likely | 25% |

**Market Impact:** If 25% tariff imposed without exemption, US spot copper could spike to $6.50–7.00/lb, while global LME price remains $5.50–6.00/lb, creating **geographic arbitrage inefficiency**. Downstream industries (construction, automotive, electric utilities) would face cost shock, potentially reducing US demand by 5–10%.

### Geopolitical Risk 6: China Smelter Overcapacity Constraining Concentrate Intake

**Background:** Chinese smelters have invested heavily in refining capacity over 2015–2025, achieving 50%+ global market share. However:

- **Mining growth has lagged smelter capacity expansion**
- **TC/RC collapse to zero/negative reflects oversupply of smelting capacity**
- **Chinese smelters now bidding aggressively for concentrate**, outbidding non-Chinese smelters

**2026 Implication:** Chinese smelter appetite for concentrate is **inelastic downward** (they cannot reduce utilization without violating capacity targets/ROI expectations). This means:

1. **Non-Chinese smelters face closure pressure** (as discussed in TC/RC analysis)
2. **Global concentrate supply chains will consolidate** around Chinese processors
3. **Miner economics improve** (they control supply to concentrated buyer base)
4. **Pricing power inverted:** Miners negotiate hard terms, smelters accept zero/negative TC/RC
