## Executive Summary

Oklo Inc. is a pre-revenue advanced nuclear company developing sodium-cooled fast microreactors (the Aurora powerhouse), nuclear fuel fabrication capabilities, and spent fuel recycling technology. The company went public via a SPAC merger with AltC Acquisition Corp in May 2024, backed by OpenAI co-founder Sam Altman (who stepped down as chairman in April 2025). Oklo generates zero revenue, has accumulated ~$130M+ in net losses since inception, and has never operated a commercial reactor — yet it commands a ~$10B market capitalization on the strength of a ~14 GW non-binding customer pipeline, a DOE-backed demonstration reactor at Idaho National Laboratory, and the narrative that AI-driven data center power demand creates an addressable market worth hundreds of billions.

**Core thesis:** Oklo is a call option on the convergence of three structural forces: (1) AI-driven power demand creating an electricity crisis that existing grid infrastructure cannot solve, (2) policy-driven nuclear renaissance (DOE Reactor Pilot Program, HALEU availability, NRC modernization), and (3) a potential first-mover advantage in nuclear fuel recycling that could transform spent fuel from a liability into feedstock. The $10B valuation prices in significant probability of successful commercialization, but the timeline risk is extreme — the first Aurora reactor targets late 2027/early 2028 operations at INL, NRC commercial licensing has not yet been granted, and Oklo's initial COLA was denied in 2022. The stock has already round-tripped from $193 to $65, suggesting the market is repricing the timeline probability after initial euphoria.

**Key upside drivers:** 14+ GW non-binding customer pipeline anchored by Switch (12 GW), Meta (1.2 GW), and Equinix (500 MW); DOE Reactor Pilot Program providing alternative authorization pathway at INL; successful completion of NRC pre-application readiness assessment with no significant gaps; $1.2B in cash and marketable securities providing multi-year runway; first-mover position in nuclear fuel recycling with $1.68B Tennessee facility announcement; and Aurora's ability to use diverse fuel sources including downblended government stockpiles and recycled fuel.

**Key downside drivers:** Zero revenue with ~$120M+ annual cash burn; $10B market cap valued entirely on optionality with no commercial track record; NRC licensing timeline uncertainty (initial COLA denied in 2022, new submission still pending); 14 GW pipeline is overwhelmingly non-binding (Switch 12 GW deal is a framework agreement); first-of-a-kind technology execution risk on a sodium-cooled fast reactor never commercially operated in the US; HALEU fuel supply chain still nascent; and 67% decline from 52-week highs suggests momentum exhaustion.

**Macro context:** Nuclear energy is experiencing a policy-driven renaissance fueled by AI data center power demand, energy security concerns, and decarbonization goals. Big Tech (Meta, Google, Microsoft, Amazon) has collectively committed to tens of GW of nuclear capacity. The DOE's Reactor Pilot Program, HALEU Availability Program, and Advanced Nuclear Fuel Line Pilot Projects provide unprecedented government support for advanced reactor deployment. However, no advanced SMR has yet delivered commercial power in the US, and the gap between announced pipelines and operational reactors remains the defining investment risk for the entire sector.

## A. Foundational Synthesis — Technology Classification & Value Chain Position

### A1. Technology & Reactor Classification

Oklo is developing the **Aurora powerhouse**, a liquid metal (sodium) cooled, metallic fuel fast neutron reactor. This technology classification is critical for understanding Oklo's positioning:

**Reactor technology:** The Aurora is a fast reactor — meaning neutrons are not moderated (slowed down) as in conventional light water reactors (LWRs). Fast neutrons enable the reactor to breed fissile material from fertile isotopes and to burn spent nuclear fuel from other reactors, creating a potential closed fuel cycle. The US operated a sodium-cooled fast reactor at INL from 1964-1994 (the Experimental Breeder Reactor-II, EBR-II), which provides the foundational technology basis for Aurora. Oklo's design draws directly on EBR-II heritage, and Oklo has access to 5 tonnes of HALEU derived from EBR-II spent fuel processing at INL.

**Size classification:**
- Aurora microreactor: 1.5 MWe (the smallest configuration)
- Aurora powerhouse: 15 MWe (modular, chainable units)
- Aurora commercial-scale: 50-75 MWe (the INL demonstration targets 75 MWe)
- Future designs: 100+ MWe under exploration for large data center deployments

The modular scaling from 1.5 MWe to 100+ MWe allows Oklo to address both remote/distributed power applications and large centralized data center campuses. However, the 75 MWe Aurora at INL will be the first unit ever built and operated — making it simultaneously a demonstration reactor, a licensing reference, and a commercial proof-of-concept.

**Fuel type:** Aurora uses metallic uranium fuel (uranium-zirconium alloy), which is denser, more thermally conductive, and easier to reprocess than the ceramic uranium oxide fuel used in conventional LWRs. The fuel requires High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium (HALEU) enriched to between 5-20% U-235, compared to the <5% enrichment of conventional reactor fuel. This creates both a specification moat (few suppliers) and a supply chain vulnerability (HALEU production is nascent in the US).

**Value chain position:** Oklo is pursuing vertical integration across the nuclear fuel cycle:

| Value Chain Layer | Oklo's Position | Status |
|-------------------|----------------|--------|
| Fuel enrichment (HALEU) | Partnership with Centrus Energy | MOU signed; Centrus operating pilot HALEU facility |
| Fuel fabrication | Aurora Fuel Fabrication Facility (A3F) at INL | DOE NSDA approved; under construction |
| Reactor design & licensing | Aurora powerhouse | Pre-application phase with NRC; DOE authorization at INL |
| Reactor construction & operation | Build-own-operate model | Ground broken at INL Sept 2025; target ops late 2027-2028 |
| Power sales | PPAs with data center customers | 14+ GW non-binding pipeline |
| Spent fuel recycling | Pyroprocessing technology | End-to-end demo completed; $1.68B Tennessee facility announced |

This level of vertical integration is unique among advanced nuclear companies. NuScale designs and licenses reactors but doesn't own/operate them or process fuel. TerraPower builds reactors but doesn't recycle fuel. X-energy makes TRISO fuel but doesn't operate plants. Oklo's ambition to control the entire fuel cycle — from fabrication to power generation to spent fuel recycling — is either a visionary competitive moat or an execution complexity multiplier, depending on one's risk tolerance.

He holds a BS in nuclear engineering from Penn State and studied nuclear science and engineering at MIT. Caroline DeWitte serves as COO. ## B. Nuclear Economics — The Licensing-to-Revenue Bridge

### B1. Licensing & Regulatory Moat

Nuclear regulatory licensing is the highest barrier to entry in the energy industry. The NRC licensing process creates a multi-year, multi-hundred-million-dollar qualification cycle that, once completed, provides extraordinary competitive protection:

**Oklo's licensing timeline:**

| Milestone | Date | Status |
|-----------|------|--------|
| First COLA submitted (Aurora micro, INL) | March 2020 | Denied (Jan 2022) — insufficient RAI responses |
| Licensing Project Plan resubmitted | September 2022 | Accepted |
| Pre-application readiness assessment (Phase 1) | March–July 2025 | Completed — no significant gaps |
| Principal Design Criteria topical report | 2025 | Accepted for accelerated NRC review; evaluation expected early 2026 |
| Phase 1 COLA submission (siting + environmental) | 2025 (targeted) | In progress |
| Full COLA approval | TBD | No firm timeline; multi-year process expected |
| DOE authorization (INL, parallel track) | Ongoing | NSDA approved; DOE pathway may enable INL operations before NRC commercial license |

**The 2022 denial is a material data point.** The NRC denied Oklo's initial application for "failure to provide information in response to NRC staff requests for additional information." This was the first-ever advanced reactor COLA submission and the first denial. Oklo has since substantially revamped its licensing approach, completed a successful readiness assessment, and benefited from NRC modernization efforts. But the denial establishes that regulatory risk is not theoretical — it has already materialized once.

**The DOE parallel pathway is strategically critical.** The DOE Reactor Pilot Program allows Oklo to build and operate the Aurora-INL reactor under DOE authorization (10 CFR 830) rather than waiting for NRC commercial licensing. This creates a viable path to operating a demonstration reactor by 2027-2028 while NRC licensing proceeds on a separate track. Successful DOE-authorized operation would provide operational data that strengthens subsequent NRC applications and de-risks customer confidence.

**Repeatable licensing strategy:** Once the first Aurora COLA is approved, subsequent applications can reference it, requiring NRC review only of site-specific and design-specific variations. This creates a compounding licensing moat — each approval makes the next one faster and cheaper. The question is whether the first approval takes 3 years or 7 years from here.

### B2. Power Purchase Agreement Economics

Oklo's revenue model is power-as-a-service: build, own, operate reactor fleets and sell electricity through long-term PPAs. This is analogous to a regulated utility's rate base model, but with several key differences:

**Pricing power:** Data center customers are paying premium prices for firm, 24/7, zero-carbon baseload power. Current nuclear PPA pricing for advanced reactors is estimated at $80-$150/MWh, significantly above grid wholesale rates but competitive with behind-the-meter generation and attractive to hyperscalers who value reliability, carbon-free attributes, and dedicated supply. Oklo's CEO has indicated pricing that is "competitive with alternatives" without disclosing specific PPA terms.

**Contract structure:** The 14+ GW pipeline consists almost entirely of non-binding framework agreements:
- **Switch (12 GW):** Non-binding master power agreement through 2044. Establishes collaboration framework with individual binding agreements to be finalized as milestones are reached.
- **Meta (1.2 GW):** Agreement for a power campus in Pike County, Ohio. First phase targeted online as early as 2030, expanding to 1.2 GW by 2034.
- **Equinix (500 MW):** Framework agreement signed April 2025.
- **Other data center providers (750 MW+):** Including Prometheus Hyperscale (100 MW) and undisclosed partners.

**The conversion gap:** The distance between 14 GW of non-binding pipeline and $0 of revenue is the defining feature of Oklo's financial profile. Converting even 1% of this pipeline (140 MW) to operating PPAs would represent a transformational inflection, but requires successfully building and operating reactors first. No non-binding agreement creates any obligation for the customer to purchase power.

### B3. Fuel Supply Chain Economics

**HALEU dependency:** Aurora reactors require HALEU (5-20% enriched uranium), which is currently produced in the US only at Centrus Energy's pilot facility in Piketon, Ohio. Global HALEU supply is dominated by Russia (via Tenex), creating a critical dependency that US policy is actively working to resolve.

Oklo's HALEU supply strategy has multiple layers:
- **INL allocation:** 5 tonnes of HALEU from EBR-II spent fuel processing, sufficient for the demonstration reactor
- **Centrus partnership:** MOU for deconversion of HALEU from UF6 to metallic form and fuel fabrication
- **DOE HALEU Availability Program:** Competitive access to government-produced HALEU
- **Fuel recycling:** Long-term strategy to close the fuel cycle by reprocessing spent fuel into new HALEU

**Fuel fabrication (A3F):** The Aurora Fuel Fabrication Facility at INL has received DOE NSDA approval — the first under the Fuel Line Pilot Projects. This facility will produce the metallic uranium fuel assemblies for Aurora reactors. Oklo was selected alongside Terrestrial Energy, TRISO-X, and Valar Atomics for the DOE program.

**Fuel recycling ($1.68B Tennessee facility):** Oklo has completed the first end-to-end demonstration of its pyroprocessing technology in collaboration with Argonne National Lab and INL. The process converts spent ceramic oxide fuel to metallic form via electrorefining in molten salt. The September 2025 announcement of a $1.68B Advanced Fuel Center in Tennessee positions Oklo to commercialize fuel recycling as a standalone revenue stream — potentially charging utilities to take their spent fuel (negative feedstock cost) and producing HALEU for its own reactors.

> **IDP: Fuel Recycling as a Hidden Option.** The market prices Oklo primarily as a reactor company, but the fuel recycling technology may be equally or more valuable. The US has ~90,000 metric tons of spent nuclear fuel with no permanent disposal solution. If Oklo can commercially recycle even a fraction of this inventory, it creates: (1) a waste-to-fuel arbitrage (paid to take spent fuel, produces valuable HALEU), (2) a captive fuel supply for its own reactors that bypasses the HALEU supply chain bottleneck, and (3) a potential solution to the US nuclear waste disposal impasse. The $1.68B Tennessee facility announcement signals management intent to commercialize this option, but the technology is unproven at industrial scale and NRC/DOE licensing for commercial fuel recycling will be its own multi-year process.

### B4. Unit Economics Framework (Projected, Not Realized)

Since Oklo has no operating revenue, unit economics must be modeled from projected parameters:

| Parameter | Estimated Range | Source/Basis |
|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| Aurora powerhouse output | 50-75 MWe | INL demonstration design spec |
| Capacity factor (target) | 90-95% | Based on EBR-II operating history |
| Annual MWh per 75 MWe unit | ~590,000-625,000 MWh | 75 MW × 8,760 hrs × 90-95% CF |
| PPA price assumption | $80-$150/MWh | Industry estimates for advanced nuclear |
| Annual revenue per unit | $47M-$94M | Range reflects PPA price uncertainty |
| Construction cost per unit | Unknown (est. $500M-$1B+) | No disclosed CapEx per unit; FOAK premium expected |
| Operating cost per unit | Unknown | No operating history |
| Fuel cost per unit | Unknown | HALEU pricing not disclosed; recycling economics TBD |
| Refueling interval | ~10 years | Company-stated design target |

The 10-year refueling interval, if achieved, would be a transformational economic advantage — conventional reactors require refueling every 18-24 months, creating significant downtime and fuel cost. However, this capability has never been demonstrated at commercial scale.

**The critical unknown is construction cost.** The Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) for Oklo's Aurora depends overwhelmingly on per-unit construction cost, which is unknown and carries extreme first-of-a-kind (FOAK) uncertainty. If construction costs per MWe exceed conventional nuclear ($6,000-$12,000/kWe for recent US builds), the PPA economics become challenging even at premium data center pricing. If Oklo achieves modular/factory-production cost reductions (targeting $3,000-$5,000/kWe at scale), the economics become compelling.

## C. Ecosystem & Competitive Moat Analysis

### C1. Customer Lock-In Architecture

**Data center co-development (emerging, not established):** Oklo's PPA model creates potential long-term customer lock-in once reactors are operational — a 20+ year PPA with a nuclear plant that provides dedicated baseload power is the ultimate switching cost. However, zero PPAs are currently operational, so the lock-in is theoretical.

**Site development lock-in:** Once Oklo deploys reactors at a customer site (e.g., Meta's Pike County campus), the physical infrastructure and NRC site-specific licensing create permanent switching costs. The reactor can't be moved, and the customer has limited alternatives for replacing that capacity.

**Fuel cycle lock-in:** If Oklo's fuel recycling capability matures, customers who send spent fuel to Oklo create a relationship that deepens over time. Oklo becomes both power provider and waste management solution — a dual lock-in.

But if AI capex decelerates (due to model efficiency improvements, recession, or regulatory constraints), the urgency for new nuclear capacity diminishes, and non-binding agreements evaporate.

> **IDP: $10B Valuation on 99% Non-Binding Data Center Pipeline.** Oklo's valuation is almost entirely a function of the market's belief that AI data center power demand will sustain current growth trajectories for 10-20 years and that Oklo will capture a meaningful share. The Switch 12 GW deal alone — which is non-binding — accounts for ~85% of the pipeline. If Switch's data center buildout slows, downsizes, or switches to an alternative power source, Oklo's pipeline compression would be dramatic. The concentrated, non-binding nature of the pipeline makes Oklo essentially a leveraged bet on AI capex growth.

## E. Supply Chain & Regulatory Risk Architecture

### E1. HALEU Supply Chain Vulnerability

HALEU is the single most critical supply chain dependency for Oklo and the broader advanced reactor sector:

| Supplier | Status | Capacity | Risk |
|----------|--------|----------|------|
| Centrus Energy (Piketon, OH) | Pilot production (NRC licensed) | ~900 kg/yr (pilot scale) | Only NRC-licensed US HALEU producer; scaling required |
| DOE (INL, EBR-II material) | Available for demo | ~10 tonnes total | Sufficient for INL demo only; not scalable |
| Urenco | Pursuing HALEU capability | TBD | European-based; timeline uncertain |
| Russia (Tenex) | Available but sanctioned | Large | US policy goal is eliminating Russian nuclear fuel dependency |

The HALEU bottleneck is the most commonly cited risk for the entire advanced nuclear sector. If HALEU supply doesn't scale to commercial volumes by 2029-2030, even successfully licensed reactors may not be able to fuel commercial deployments beyond demonstration units.

Oklo's fuel recycling capability is a strategic hedge against this bottleneck — if pyroprocessing can convert existing spent fuel into HALEU feedstock, Oklo partially decouples from the enrichment supply chain.

### E2. NRC Licensing Risk

**The NRC is the gating factor for commercial deployment.** Key risks:

- **Prior denial:** The 2022 COLA denial demonstrates NRC's willingness to reject applications it deems inadequate. While Oklo has substantially improved its approach, the precedent is chilling.
- **Timeline uncertainty:** No advanced non-LWR reactor has completed NRC licensing. NuScale's design certification took ~6 years (2017-2023) for a conventional LWR-derived design. Oklo's sodium-cooled fast reactor is more novel, potentially requiring longer review.
- **Staffing and bandwidth:** The NRC is simultaneously processing applications from NuScale, Kairos, X-energy, TerraPower, and others. Agency bandwidth constraints could extend timelines.

**The DOE parallel pathway partially mitigates NRC risk** by allowing INL operations under DOE authorization, but commercial deployment to customer sites (Switch, Meta, Equinix) requires NRC licensing that cannot be bypassed.

### E3. Political & Policy Risk

Oklo benefits from bipartisan support for nuclear energy — the ADVANCE Act, DOE Reactor Pilot Program, and HALEU programs have received broad congressional backing. However:

- **NRC leadership changes:** Regulatory philosophy shifts with administration changes
- **DOE funding continuity:** Reactor Pilot Program, Fuel Line Pilot Projects depend on appropriations
- **State-level nuclear policy:** Some states have nuclear construction moratoria that could limit deployment geography
- **Nuclear waste policy:** Fuel recycling requires regulatory frameworks that don't fully exist yet; NRC/DOE jurisdiction for commercial reprocessing is uncertain

> **IDP: The DOE Parallel Pathway Is More Important Than the Market Realizes.** The DOE authorization route at INL allows Oklo to build and operate a reactor potentially 3-5 years faster than the NRC commercial licensing path. Successful DOE-authorized operation creates operational data, customer confidence, and revenue generation while NRC licensing continues. If the INL reactor operates successfully under DOE oversight, it transforms the NRC application from a theoretical design review into a demonstrated technology assessment — fundamentally changing the probability of NRC approval. The DOE pathway may be the most underappreciated de-risking mechanism in Oklo's strategy.

## G. Pattern Matching & IDP Flagging

> **IDP 1: $10B Market Cap on $0 Revenue — Priced for Perfection.** Oklo's valuation implies aggressive probability-weighted outcomes across multiple options (reactor success, pipeline conversion, fuel recycling). The 67% decline from the $193 52-week high to $65 suggests the market is already repricing some of these probabilities downward. Either Oklo is dramatically overvalued relative to NuScale, or NuScale is dramatically undervalued, or the market correctly prices Oklo's business model optionality as genuinely worth more than NuScale's regulatory head start.

> **IDP 3: Non-Binding Pipeline Inflation.** The 14+ GW pipeline headline is dominated by a single 12 GW non-binding framework agreement with Switch. This round-trip destroyed significant retail investor capital and creates an overhang of trapped holders looking to sell on any recovery toward their cost basis.
