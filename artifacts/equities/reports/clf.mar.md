## A. Foundational Synthesis — Material Classification & Value Chain Position

### A1. Material Portfolio Classification

Cleveland-Cliffs is the largest flat-rolled steel producer in North America and the **only domestic producer of grain-oriented electrical steel (GOES)** — a specialty material critical to transformers, power grid infrastructure, EV motors, and defense electrical systems. The company controls an unusually complete vertical value chain:

**Specialty Materials — Electrical Steels (GOES + Non-Grain-Oriented/NOES):**
- GOES is used in transformer cores where magnetic permeability and low core loss are essential. Every utility-scale transformer, distribution transformer, and large power transformer in the US requires GOES. The material specification is defined by ASTM A876 and international IEC 60404 standards.
- NOES (non-grain-oriented electrical steel) is used in EV traction motors, generators, and rotating equipment.
- CLF produces GOES at Butler Works (PA) and Zanesville (OH). These are the **only GOES production facilities in the United States**.
- Qualification cycle: 6-18 months for transformer OEMs. Switching requires re-qualification of core loss characteristics, dimensional tolerances, and coating compatibility.
- Cost-of-failure ratio: GOES represents approximately 30-40% of a distribution transformer's material cost, but transformer failure causes grid outages costing $50K-$500K+ per hour for utility customers. The criticality is extreme.

**Flat-Rolled Carbon Steels (Commodity Overlay):**
- Hot-rolled coil (HRC), cold-rolled (CR), hot-dip galvanized (HDG), electrogalvanized (EG), tin mill, plate.
- CLF is the largest US supplier of automotive-grade steels, serving every major North American automaker.
- Carbon steel is commodity-priced (indexed to HRC spot), unlike GOES which carries specification-based pricing.

**Raw Material Inputs (Vertically Integrated):**
- CLF owns its entire iron ore supply through mining operations in Minnesota and Michigan (Tilden, Hibbing, Minorca, United Taconite).
- Owns cokemaking and direct reduction iron (DRI) facilities, reducing dependence on external metallurgical coal markets.
- Following the Stelco acquisition (2024), added Canadian steelmaking capacity with access to Great Lakes logistics.

> **IDP: The Dual-Personality Problem.** Cleveland-Cliffs is simultaneously a commodity steel company (75%+ of revenue) and a specialty materials monopolist (GOES). The market prices CLF as a commodity steel cyclical at ~5x EBITDA, while GOES alone would merit a 10-14x multiple as a sole-source critical infrastructure material. This valuation disconnect is the core of the investment thesis.

### A2. Leadership Pedigree & Strategic Continuity

**CEO Lourenco Goncalves** has led Cleveland-Cliffs since August 2014. He is a metallurgical engineer by training (Federal University of Minas Gerais, Brazil) with 30+ years in the steel industry. He orchestrated CLF's transformation from an iron ore miner into a vertically integrated steel company through a series of acquisitions:
- AK Steel (2020) — brought GOES capability and automotive steel
- ArcelorMittal USA assets (2020) — added blast furnace capacity
- Stelco (2024, $2.5B) — added Canadian capacity and Great Lakes logistics

**Insider alignment:** As of February 11, 2026, Goncalves owns approximately 3.0 million shares. However, he sold 3.0 million shares on February 11, 2026 at ~$12.41/share ($37.2M transaction). He subsequently received 650,900 RSUs and 650,900 performance-based MSUs vesting over three years (February 18, 2026). The large insider sale is a caution flag, though it was partially offset by new equity grants tied to performance targets.

> **IDP: CEO Insider Sale at Cyclical Low.** Goncalves selling 3M shares at ~$12.41 — near the cyclical trough after a terrible 2025 — is a negative signal. However, the simultaneous grant of 1.3M performance-linked units suggests management and the board expect a recovery. This tension warrants monitoring.

## B. Materials Economics — The Specification-to-Revenue Bridge

### B1. Specification & Qualification Moat — GOES

GOES is not a commodity. It is a precision-engineered silicon steel with specific crystallographic orientation (Goss texture) that concentrates magnetic flux in the rolling direction. Key specifications include:
- Core loss (W/kg at 1.7T/50Hz): grades range from conventional (M6) to high-permeability (Hi-B, HiB-LS)
- Magnetic induction (Tesla at 800 A/m)
- Insulation coating compatibility (C-5, C-6 types)
- Dimensional tolerance: strip thickness 0.23mm to 0.35mm, width up to 1000mm

**Qualification moat:** Transformer manufacturers must qualify GOES supply through extensive core loss testing, stacking factor validation, and coating adhesion testing. Switching suppliers requires 6-18 months of re-qualification. Once qualified, customers face enormous switching costs because transformer designs are optimized around specific GOES grade performance curves.

**Sole-source position:** CLF is the **only** US producer of GOES. The only alternatives are imported GOES from Nippon Steel (Japan), POSCO (South Korea), Baosteel (China), and ThyssenKrupp (Germany) — all subject to Section 232 tariffs at **50%** plus historical anti-dumping/countervailing duties ranging from 30-180% depending on origin.

The effective tariff wall on imported GOES into the US ranges from **80% to 230%**, making CLF the de facto monopoly supplier for any buyer that cannot absorb those costs.

### B2. Pricing Power & Value-Based Pricing

CLF operates a **dual pricing model:**

**Carbon steels (commodity):** Priced on HRC index, contract, or spot. Current HRC prices are approximately $900-950/ton, supported by 50% Section 232 tariffs that create a domestic price floor roughly 2x international pricing (international HRC ~$450-500/ton). CLF commands slight premiums to spot due to automotive qualification and service center relationships.

**Electrical steels (specialty):** GOES pricing is opaque but estimated at $2,000-4,000/ton depending on grade (conventional vs. Hi-B), significantly above carbon steel pricing. GOES pricing is less volatile than HRC because it is specification-driven and the tariff wall eliminates import competition for most applications.

**ASP trajectory:** Management guided Q1 2026 average selling prices up ~$60/ton versus Q4 2025, driven by:
- Termination of a below-market ArcelorMittal slab contract that was value-destructive in 2025
- Q1 contract resets at higher prices
- Improved product mix toward higher-margin automotive and electrical steels

> **IDP: The $500M Slab Contract Tailwind.** The expiration of an index-based slab supply contract with ArcelorMittal — which management called "value-destructive" — is estimated to add approximately $500 million in incremental EBITDA. This is a non-recurring structural improvement, not a cyclical bounce. The market appears to be underweighting this.

### B3. Raw Material Input Economics

CLF's vertical integration is its primary cost advantage:
- **Iron ore:** Self-supplied from owned mines. ~60% of total iron ore needs. Eliminates exposure to seaborne iron ore price volatility.
- **Metallurgical coal/coke:** Partially self-supplied through owned cokemaking operations. Remainder purchased on contract.
- **Scrap steel:** Used in EAF operations. Purchased at market but hedgeable.
- **Energy:** Natural gas is a significant input for DRI and blast furnace operations. Exposed to Henry Hub pricing.

**Passthrough mechanism:** CLF uses a combination of index-based pricing (tied to CRU HRC index), contract pricing with escalation clauses, and surcharge mechanisms for energy and alloy costs. Automotive contracts typically reset annually or semi-annually with base price + surcharge structures.

### B4. Gross Margin Bridge

Full-year 2025 was disastrous: adjusted EBITDA of just $37M on $18.6B revenue (~0.2% margin). Decomposing why:
- **Volume:** Automotive production weakness throughout 2025 reduced shipments and fixed cost absorption.
- **Mix:** Higher proportion of lower-margin spot sales as contract volumes declined.
- **Slab contract drag:** The ArcelorMittal slab contract consumed an estimated $500M+ of EBITDA.
- **Canadian tariff impact:** 25% retaliatory Canadian duties on Stelco exports hurt recently acquired assets.
- **Raw material lag:** Input cost increases outpaced contract price resets in H1 2025.

**2026 bridge to recovery:**
- ASP up $60/ton in Q1 (guided)
- Cost down $10/ton full-year (guided, with $20/ton up in Q1 normalizing in Q2)
- Slab contract expiration: +$500M EBITDA
- Automotive recovery: gradual improvement in NA production
- Stelco integration: management says Stelco now "contributing" after being "disappointing" in 2025
- POSCO equity investment: $700M cash infusion for deleveraging

## C. Ecosystem & Competitive Moat Analysis

### C1. Customer Lock-In Architecture

**Automotive (28-30% of revenue):** CLF is the largest supplier of automotive-grade steel in North America, serving every major OEM. Automotive steel qualification cycles are 6-12 months, and OEMs are reluctant to switch suppliers mid-platform. CLF's Advanced High-Strength Steel (AHSS) and Ultra High-Strength Steel (UHSS) products are designed into specific vehicle platforms.

**Electrical steel (undisclosed % but critical):** GOES customers (transformer OEMs like ABB, Siemens, Eaton, Hitachi Energy) are locked in by qualification, tariff protection, and the absence of domestic alternatives.

**Infrastructure & manufacturing (30%):** Includes construction, appliance, pipe & tube. Less sticky than automotive or electrical, but tariff protection creates domestic supply preference.

### C2. Competitive Landscape

| Competitor | Ticker | Revenue | EV/EBITDA | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nucor | NUE | ~$34B | ~8x | Largest US EAF producer. Lower cost structure. No GOES. |
| Steel Dynamics | STLD | ~$18B | ~7x | Lean EAF operator. Higher margins. No GOES. |
| US Steel | X | ~$12B | ~6x | Integrated. Nippon Steel acquisition saga. No GOES. |
| CLF | CLF | $18.6B | ~5x* | Only US GOES producer. Highest leverage. Automotive leader. |

*CLF's ~5x EBITDA is based on forward estimates; trailing is essentially infinite due to near-zero 2025 EBITDA.

**Critical differentiator:** Neither Nucor, Steel Dynamics, nor US Steel produce GOES. CLF's GOES monopoly is not replicable — building a GOES production line requires $500M-$1B+ investment, 3-5 years construction, and decades of metallurgical know-how in Goss texture control. The DOE awarded CLF a $75M grant specifically to support GOES capacity at Butler Works, recognizing the national security dimension.

### C3. End-Market Diversification & Cyclicality

**Revenue mix (FY2025 steelmaking revenue of $18.0B):**
- Infrastructure & manufacturing: $5.4B (30%) — partially cyclical but benefiting from infrastructure spending
- Distributors & converters: $5.2B (29%) — spot-market exposed, most cyclical
- Automotive: $5.0B (28%) — cyclical but contract-based with qualification lock-in
- Steel producers (slab/semi-finished): $2.3B (13%) — declining post-contract expiration

The mix is reasonably diversified but heavily exposed to cyclical end markets. The secular growth kicker — GOES for grid modernization and data center power infrastructure — is not separately disclosed but is growing in strategic importance.

## E. Supply Chain & Geopolitical Risk Architecture

### E1. Critical Mineral / Material Dependency

GOES production requires specific silicon steel grades (3-3.5% silicon content) produced through complex thermomechanical processing (hot rolling, cold rolling, decarburization annealing, secondary recrystallization). The key inputs are:
- Iron ore (self-supplied)
- Silicon (sourced primarily from domestic suppliers; some import exposure)
- Magnesia oxide (MgO) coating materials
- Natural gas (energy-intensive annealing process)

CLF's GOES production is **not dependent on USGS Critical Minerals List materials** — a significant advantage over rare earth magnet producers. The primary input risk is energy cost (natural gas) and the highly specialized equipment/know-how required for Goss texture control.

### E2. Customer Concentration & Program Risk

- No single customer >10% of revenue (disclosed)
- Automotive platform risk: if a major OEM (e.g., Stellantis, which has been weak) reduces production, CLF's automotive volumes decline
- GOES customer concentration is higher but undisclosed — the major US transformer OEMs (ABB/Hitachi Energy, Siemens, Eaton, Howard Industries) collectively represent the entire domestic GOES market

### E3. Geopolitical & Trade Policy Exposure

**Tailwinds:**
- Section 232 tariffs at 50% on steel imports: creates domestic price floor ~2x international
- Anti-dumping/CVD duties on GOES imports: 30-180% depending on origin
- DOE transformer efficiency standards (compliance by April 2029): mandate Hi-B GOES, which CLF produces domestically
- DOE awarded CLF $75M grant for Butler Works GOES capacity (though Trump admin cancelled portions of a larger $500M award)
- Data center buildout creating unprecedented transformer demand

**Headwinds:**
- 25% Canadian retaliatory tariffs impacted Stelco export profitability in 2025
- Tariff policy uncertainty: any relaxation of Section 232 would compress domestic premiums
- US steel prices at ~2x international levels are politically vulnerable
