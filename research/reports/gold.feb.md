## Executive Summary

Gold trading near $5,036/oz in February 2026 represents 17% year-to-date gains and 71.9% appreciation over the trailing twelve months. The metal has delivered unprecedented value dynamics across fundamental vectors: total demand exceeded 5,000 tonnes for the first time in 2025 (generating $555bn notional value, +45% y/y), central banks accumulated 863t, and investment vehicles (ETFs, bars/coins) captured over 50% of total offtake. Yet accelerating supply from recycling (rising 3% despite price surge to $1,605/oz AISC) and speculative net long liquidation (-85,634 contracts in three weeks to 165,604 contracts, the lowest since May 2025) create technical headwinds. This report synthesizes the dual demand drivers (monetary-regime weakness + geopolitical de-dollarization), examines the marginal cost structure, and flags eight Investment Discrimination Patterns indicating near-term price vulnerability even amid structural bull theses.

## A. Foundational Synthesis — Metal Classification & Dual-Use Economics

### A1. Metal Classification & Demand Profile

Gold defies traditional commodity classification. It functions across four distinct demand silos, each responding to different economic regimes:

**Monetary/Store-of-Value:** Gold's core identity remains a permanent wealth reserve and inflation hedge. Central banks hold ~36,000 tonnes globally, with 76% of central banking officials now pledging to increase holdings over the next five years (up from 69% in 2024). This institutional positioning reflects accelerating de-dollarization: 75% of central banks intend to reduce dollar exposure (up from 62%), with BRICS bloc coordination intensifying around a gold-anchored settlement unit anchored at 40% gold weighting. In the context of 3.50–3.75% federal funds rates and 10-year TIPS yielding approximately 1.89% real, the opportunity cost of holding zero-yield bullion has compressed, re-activating the traditional "negative real rates = gold appreciation" transmission mechanism.

**Industrial/Fabrication:** Electronics, semiconductors, and medical device manufacturing consumed an estimated 280–300 tonnes annually in 2025. Q1 2025 data showed Japan at 19t (+1% y/y), South Korea at 6t (flat), and China/Hong Kong at 19t (+5% y/y). Fabrication gold markets are valued at $151.9bn (2024) and projected to reach $206.7bn by 2030 (5.3% CAGR), driven by AI compute expansion and advanced node adoption. However, margin pressure on manufacturers (operating margins compressed by record gold prices) is driving optimization efforts to reduce per-unit material content—a structural headwind on consumption per unit shipped.

**Jewelry:** Jewelry offtake collapsed during 2025, with tonnage down year-on-year across all major regions despite nominal spending gains. At elevated price levels ($4,900–$5,600/oz), retail consumers show clear price elasticity: lower tonnage volumes at higher prices. This marks a critical demand vector: jewelry represents ~2,000 tonnes annually at lower price regimes, but becomes demand-destructive above $5,200/oz in developed markets.

**Emerging/Green Energy:** Medical devices, aerospace, and next-generation energy applications (hydrogen electrolyzers, fuel cells, photovoltaic back-contacts) represent forward-looking demand vectors. These remain small in absolute terms (<50t annually) but are inflection points for long-cycle industrial restructuring. Supply scarcity in these end-markets would compress into price premiums on refined cathode-grade material.

### A2. Investment Vehicle Landscape

Global gold ETF assets reached a record $669bn AUM (31 January 2026), with 4,145 tonnes in collective holdings—also an all-time high. SPDR Gold Shares (GLD) commands $173.3bn in assets, representing approximately 26% of global ETF gold holdings. ETF inflows have stabilized at 801 tonnes for full-year 2025 (second strongest on record), versus the critical observation that net ETF outflows combined with rising spot prices historically signal demand desperation (IDP Flag #1).

Bar and coin investment reached a 12-year high in 2025, with combined bar/coin/ETF demand now accounting for >50% of total offtake (versus <33% in 2024). This represents a structural shift in the composition of demand—away from producer end-use and toward financial engineering. Price inelasticity of investment demand at $5,000+ levels remains unproven; historical precedent suggests elasticity increases sharply above $2,000/oz nominal.

## B. Supply/Demand Fundamentals — The Physical Balance Sheet

### B1. Supply Architecture

**Mine Production:** Global mine production reached record 3,700+ tonnes in 2025, with growth concentrated among low-cost, high-volume producers. China leads at 380.2t annually (2025 estimate: 372t for 2026), followed by Russia at 330t, Australia at 320t, United States at 180t, and Canada at 150t. The top-5 producer bloc commands ~55% of global output. Supply growth has moderated to +1% y/y in 2025 (reaching 5,002t total supply, the highest since 1970 in World Gold Council data), indicating marginal new capacity coming online is limited.

All-in Sustaining Costs (AISC) rose 9% year-over-year to $1,605/oz in Q3 2025, driven by labor inflation, energy cost escalation (particularly in low-cost African and Asian jurisdictions), and incremental environmental compliance costs. At current AISC levels, marginal producers operating in tier-two jurisdictions (Peru, Mexico, Ghana) face margin compression; any spot price pullback below $2,200/oz would force production deferrals or closures at ~15–20% of global capacity.

**Recycling/Secondary Supply:** Recycled supply increased only 3% in 2025 despite gold prices rising from $2,000 to $4,500–$5,100/oz range. This severely sub-expected elasticity suggests behavioral constraints: jewelry recycling requires cultural/religious compliance in key markets (India, Middle East, China), scrap streams are fragmented, and refining infrastructure has not scaled with price appreciation. Recycled supply is estimated at 1,100–1,200 tonnes annually. Elevated gold prices ought to drive 15–20% supply elasticity; the observed 3% suggests recycling supply remains structurally price-inelastic below $7,000/oz.

**Government/Institutional Sales:** Central bank and IMF sales have been near-zero to negative (net buyers) for 15+ years. This structural floor on institutional supply is a critical bull market foundation—no regime shock would trigger rapid gold dumping.

**By-Product Dynamics:** Copper, silver, zinc, and molybdenum mining operations generate gold as a by-product (~300–400t annually). By-product gold is often treated as a cost-reduction credit in AISC calculations; any slowdown in base metal mining would compress total supply. Current copper market tightness and zinc shortages suggest by-product gold supply remains well-supported.

### B2. Demand Decomposition

**Industrial Fabrication:** 280–300 tonnes annually; demand growth constrained by price elasticity above $1,800/oz. Semiconductor demand is diversifying away from simple interconnect gold toward advanced materials (nano-gold catalysts, gold-platinum alloys for high-temperature applications). Absolute tonnage is stable but margin-sensitive.

**Investment Demand:**
- ETFs: +801t net inflows (2025), record $669bn AUM
- Bar & Coin: +860t (12-year high)
- Combined Investment: 1,660+ tonnes, representing 33% of total demand

Investment demand is driven by macro regime collapse (negative real rates, geopolitical uncertainty, central bank policy divergence). Saturation dynamics remain unclear; ETF net inflows have not declined despite $3,000 price appreciation from mid-2024.

**Jewelry/Silverware:** 1,600–2,000 tonnes, highly price-elastic. At >$5,000/oz, jewelry demand is compressed; demand destruction likely resumes if prices hold above $5,200/oz for sustained periods.

**Central Bank/Sovereign:** 863 tonnes in 2025 (upper range of historical 600–900t expectation). Poland, China, Turkey, Kazakhstan, and India were dominant buyers. Central bank demand is driven by de-dollarization consensus (BRICS expansion, currency reserve recomposition, geopolitical sanctions risk). This demand vector is decoupled from price and represents a multi-year structural shift.

**Emerging Demand Vectors:** Hydrogen electrolysis, fuel cell catalysts, photovoltaic applications, and advanced aerospace applications represent forward-demand growth areas, but current consumption is negligible (~20–40t annually). Long-cycle infrastructure build-out (2027–2032 timeframe) could unlock 200–400t incremental demand in these verticals.

### B3. Inventory & Warehouse Dynamics

**GLD ETF Holdings:** 4,145 tonnes as of 31 January 2026, representing $209.3bn notional value at $5,036/oz spot prices. GLD net inflows have averaged 15–20 tonnes/week, indicating continued institutional accumulation. The significance: GLD provides transparent, auditable, dealer-hedge supply. Heavy GLD inflows suppress physical dealer premiums (bid-ask spreads on kilobars and 100oz bars), indicating physical market ease.

**COMEX Holdings:** Registered gold inventories at COMEX totaled 6.4 million oz (199 tonnes) as of early February 2026. Eligible inventories (unregistered but fungible) summed to approximately 8.2 million oz (255 tonnes). Total COMEX gold supply represents ~10% of global annual mine production. The critical observation: COMEX registered stocks have been stable; no sharp drawdowns have occurred (IDP Flag #2 absent), suggesting no physical tightness at the futures contract level.

**LBMA Vault Holdings:** The London Bullion Market Association vault network (Brinks, G4S, Malca-Amit, others) holds approximately 8,000–9,500 tonnes, representing the global physical settlement hub for OTC precious metals trading. Vault allocations have been stable; no storage cost inflation or waitlist formation evident in the market.

**Central Bank Reserves:** Combined sovereign gold reserves total approximately 36,000 tonnes. BRICS bloc holdings exceed 6,000 tonnes (20–21% of global central bank reserves), with Russia at 2,336t and China at 2,298t. Incremental central bank buying has not driven reserve accumulation volatility; purchases are absorbed through mine production and scrap recycling.

## C. Macro Regime & Price Driver Analysis

### C1. Monetary Policy & Real Rate Sensitivity

The Federal Reserve has maintained the federal funds target range at 3.50–3.75% (January 2026 decision). Market expectations now price at most two additional rate cuts through 2026 and none in 2027, representing a structural shift from the late-2023 cutting cycle. This stasis in nominal rates, combined with CPI prints of 2.4–2.7% (January 2026), generates 10-year TIPS real yields of approximately 1.89%.

The critical insight: gold has historically rallied sharply when real rates turn negative or approach zero. At +1.89% real on 10Y TIPS, gold faces structural headwinds. The traditional transmission mechanism—negative real rates trigger portfolio rebalancing toward zero-yield precious metals—is impaired. Gold's $5,000+ rally from $2,000 (2021 trough) to current levels has been driven by non-rate factors (geopolitical risk premium, de-dollarization, inflation expectations volatility, ETF inflows), not fundamental rate dynamics.

**Fed Independence Risk:** Recent policy divergence between the Fed and Treasury/Congress (regarding asset purchase frameworks, bank lending standards, and fiscal dominance) has created narrative uncertainty. Central bank independence is a forward macro watch; any perceived Fed capitulation to fiscal pressures would weaken dollar credibility and accelerate gold pricing upward. Consensus expects 2–3 cuts in late 2026 if recession concerns mount.

**Dollar Strength (DXY):** The US Dollar Index traded near 96.84 on 10 February 2026, representing an approximate 4% decline from late-2025 highs near 101. Year-to-date dollar weakness has supported gold prices (+17% YTD). Over the trailing 12 months, DXY has depreciated 10.3%, marking a significant structural shift in dollar demand. Currency depreciation reflects:

- Treasury supply saturation (Chinese pension funds reducing Treasury allocations, European central banks diversifying reserves)
- BRICS de-dollarization acceleration (BRICS+ pilot gold-anchored settlement unit launched October 2025)
- Real rate compression globally (ECB at -0.25%, Bank of Japan at +0.25%)

The dollar weakness is a major gold bull catalyst; any reversal (Fed tightening surprise, growth re-acceleration, flight-to-safety USD demand) would immediately pressure precious metals.

**Inflation Expectations & Breakeven Analysis:** 5-year breakeven inflation is trading near 2.40–2.60%, suggesting markets are pricing stable price-level dynamics over the medium term. This is below the 3.0–3.5% realized inflation of 2021–2023. Gold's rally is not inflation-expectation driven; rather, it reflects uncertainty around inflation volatility and central bank coordination breakdown.

**M2 Dynamics:** Broad money supply growth has stabilized at 2–3% annual rates (down from 5.4% in 2021), indicating monetary tightening after the pandemic/stimulus cycle. Slower M2 growth is typically a gold headwind; gold rallies with monetary acceleration, not deceleration. The 2025–2026 period shows no M2 surprise to the upside, yet gold has rallied 70%+, suggesting non-monetary drivers are dominant.

### C2. Geopolitical & Safe-Haven Dynamics

De-dollarization is advancing as a measurable geopolitical phenomenon. BRICS+ expansion (adding five new members in 2024: Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, UAE) has created a coalition representing ~36% of global GDP (nominal) and 50%+ of global population. The October 2025 BRICS pilot of a gold-anchored settlement "Unit" at 40% gold / 60% basket weighting represents a credible step toward reserve asset diversification.

Central bank gold purchasing has accelerated to record levels: 1,045 tonnes in 2024, 863 tonnes in 2025, and annualized forecasts suggesting 900–1,100 tonnes in 2026. This represents a structural 2–2.5x increase over the 2010–2019 average of 400–500t annually. The shift is driven by:

- Perceived dollar over-extension and geopolitical sanctions (Russian gold, Iranian assets, Chinese foreign exchange)
- Monetary policy divergence and central bank credibility doubts
- Regional trade settlement preferences (CNY clearing, INR settlement, AED payment schemes)
- Portfolio rebalancing as US fiscal deficits and debt-to-GDP ratios rise (US Treasury market saturation)

The de-dollarization thesis is a multi-year structural catalyst for gold prices; gold prices could appreciate 30–50% if BRICS+ gold-anchored settlement becomes operational and captures 10–15% of global bilateral trade (estimated $15–20 trillion annually).

However, the countervailing risk is execution: geopolitical coalitions are notoriously difficult to operationalize, and monetary union proposals (similar to BRICS currency plans) have failed historically (failed EU-analog attempts, underdeveloped emerging market central bank coordination). If the BRICS+ initiative stalls (geopolitical misalignment, member policy divergence), the bullish de-dollarization thesis would rapidly deflate.

### C3. Green Energy & Industrial Transition Demand

Gold plays a growing role in hydrogen electrolysis (anode catalysts), proton-exchange membrane fuel cells, and photovoltaic back-contact applications. Advanced aerospace (next-generation jet engines, hypersonic vehicles) requires high-temperature gold-platinum alloys. Estimated current consumption in these verticals: 20–40 tonnes annually.

Global hydrogen production capacity is expanding (>50 GW electrolyzer capacity by 2030 target, versus ~8 GW in 2025). If gold-based electrocatalysts achieve scaling, incremental demand could reach 150–300 tonnes by 2035. This represents a 2–3% lift to total demand, but on a multi-decade timeline.

The critical caveat: alternative catalysts (nickel-iron compounds, platinum-group metal alternatives) are in advanced development. Gold's role in green energy remains contingent on competitive advantage in specific high-efficiency applications. No structural supply crunch is imminent.

## D. Financial Logic & Valuation

### Marginal Cost of Production Analysis

At $1,605/oz AISC (Q3 2025, +9% y/y), the marginal cost curve for the global gold mining industry exhibits a steep slope:

- **Tier-1 Producers** (Canada, Australia, US): $1,200–$1,350/oz AISC, generating $3,600–$3,850/oz cash profit margin
- **Tier-2 Producers** (Russia, China, Peru, Mexico): $1,450–$1,650/oz AISC, generating $3,350–$3,600/oz cash profit margin
- **Tier-3 Producers** (Ghana, Zimbabwe, Senegal, Tajikistan): $1,750–$2,000/oz AISC, generating $3,000–$3,300/oz cash profit margin

The steep profit margin gradient means that any price move below $2,200/oz spot would trigger production deferrals among tier-3 operators. Cumulative tier-3 capacity represents approximately 250–350 tonnes annually, or 7–9% of global production. A price collapse to $1,800/oz would force 20–30% capacity shutdowns, creating a natural price floor.

Conversely, at current $5,036/oz pricing, all producers are generating exceptional margin expansion. Capital expenditures are accelerating: Tier-1 producers are expanding into higher-cost but longer-reserve jurisdictions (Arctic Canada, deep-sea Peru), tier-2 producers are acquiring adjacent mineral rights, and tier-3 operators are reinvesting in processing efficiency. This capital intensity suggests supply growth could accelerate 2–3% annually through 2028, assuming prices remain above $3,500/oz.

### Relative Value vs. Other Metals

**Gold/Silver Ratio:** Currently trading near 57:1 (mid-range of historical 50–65:1 band). In May 2025, the ratio peaked at 99.7:1, representing an extreme that has now normalized. At 57:1, gold-to-silver relative value suggests neither metal is exceptionally cheap or expensive versus historical median. The ratio is mean-reverting; extremes >80:1 or <50:1 typically trigger relative-value trading flows.

**Gold/Platinum Ratio:** Gold trading at approximately 2.0x platinum pricing (versus historical 1.3–1.5x). The outsized gold premium reflects platinum's underperformance due to diesel-vehicle fleet contraction (catalytic converter demand compression) and weak jewelry recycling. Platinum upside is contingent on hydrogen economy scaling and autocatalyst demand stabilization; no near-term reversion to historical ratios is expected.

**Gold vs. Bitcoin:** Gold trades near record nominal prices; Bitcoin has oscillated between $38,000–$50,000 (February 2026 range), representing a 15–20% YTD rally versus gold's 17% YTD gain. Bitcoin's correlation to gold has declined sharply (now 0.15–0.25 rolling 30-day), suggesting Bitcoin is no longer a "digital gold" substitution story but rather a separate risk-on/macro-sentiment barometer. In deflationary shocks, gold should outperform; in inflation-acceleration regimes, Bitcoin may re-correlate.

### Real Price Adjusted for Inflation

Gold's nominal price has appreciated from ~$1,100/oz (2008 trough) to $5,036/oz (February 2026), representing 358% nominal appreciation. Adjusting for cumulative US CPI inflation (1.25x, or 25% cumulative through 2026), real price appreciation is 268%. In 1980 dollars (the prior bull market peak), gold would need to reach $7,200–$7,800/oz to achieve price parity on a real basis.

This suggests gold remains 40–50% undervalued on an inflation-adjusted basis versus prior peaks. However, this analysis assumes commodity basket inflation comparable to the 1970s spike; current disinflation dynamics suggest the real price analogy is less directional.

### Inventory-Adjusted Supply/Demand Model

Global gold inventories (excluding central bank reserves) total approximately 12,500–14,000 tonnes (ETFs, COMEX, LBMA, non-monetary jewelry, industrial scrap). Annual supply (production + recycling) is 4,800–5,000 tonnes; annual demand is 5,000+ tonnes.

At current demand levels, inventory-to-supply ratios are compressing: inventory coverage has declined from 2.8 years (2019) to 2.6 years (2026). This compression suggests marginal tightness, but is not yet acute. If investment demand were to spike further (e.g., 2,000–2,500 tonnes annually), inventory drawdowns would accelerate, and physical market contango could invert to backwardation (IDP Flag #8).

### Positioning-Based Analysis

**CFTC Net Speculative Positioning:** Large speculators hold 165,604 net long contracts (as of early February 2026), down sharply from 251,238 contracts in mid-January. This represents liquidation of 85,634 contracts in three weeks—the largest reduction since May 2025. Positioning has returned to the lowest level in 37 weeks, indicating speculative exhaustion or profit-taking.

Historically, gold has often bottomed when speculative net longs fall below 150,000 contracts; we are now approaching that threshold. Conversely, commercial short positioning remains at a 36.7% bearish score, indicating dealer/producer hedges are in place and shorts are protected. The combination of speculative longs near multi-week lows + commercial shorts intact suggests limited downside from speculative washout, but also implies any new bull move would require new long accumulation.

## E. Pattern Matching & IDP Flagging

Investment Discrimination Patterns (IDPs) serve as real-time indicators of market regime shifts. Eight critical IDPs are examined:

> **IDP: ETF Outflows with Rising Price**
> If gold prices rally 15%+ while ETF net flows turn negative (outflows), this signals demand capitulation among retail/institutional investors and suggests price rally is driven by short-covering or FOMO rather than fundamental accumulation. Current state: ETF inflows remain +801t (2025), and YTD 2026 inflows are tracking positively. IDP not active. Bull signal intact.

> **IDP: COMEX Registered Inventory Drawdowns**
> Sharp declines in COMEX-registered gold (moving from registered to eligible, or off-exchange) suggest physical tightness and potential backwardation signals. Current state: COMEX registered inventories stable at 199t (6.4M oz), eligible at 255t. No material drawdown evident. IDP not active. Liquidity appears normal.

> **IDP: Mine Production Declining Below Trend**
> Global mine production showed +1% growth in 2025, reaching record 3,700t. Recycling supply rose only 3% despite 120% cumulative price appreciation since 2021, suggesting price elasticity is weaker than expected but not negative. IDP is partially active: recycling supply elasticity is sub-trend, indicating future supply growth may undershoot expectations.

> **IDP: Gold/Silver Ratio Extremes (>80:1 or <50:1)**
> Current gold-to-silver ratio is 57:1, within the historical 50–65:1 normal range. The 99.7:1 extreme in May 2025 has normalized. IDP is dormant. No relative-value signal.

> **IDP: Real Rates Falling with Flat Gold Price**
> 10Y TIPS real yields are +1.89%, and gold is up 17% YTD. This is NOT the classic IDP pattern (real rates falling should drive gold prices higher). The absence of real-rate compression is a headwind: gold has rallied despite sticky real rates, indicating non-rate drivers dominate. IDP is inverted; bull momentum is fragile if dependent on non-fundamental flows.

> **IDP: Central Bank Buying Accelerating**
> Central bank purchases have accelerated to 863t (2025) versus 1,045t (2024), representing a structural 2x average above historical norms. BRICS de-dollarization coordination is advancing. This IDP is strongly active and represents a durable structural bull signal.

> **IDP: Industrial Demand Exceeding Supply Growth**
> Total demand (5,000+t, 2025) exceeds supply growth rates (+1% y/y). However, investment demand (1,660+t) is masking weak jewelry demand (-5% tonnage y/y) and flat industrial fabrication demand. True structural industrial demand growth is minimal; demand growth is financial engineering, not end-use. IDP is partially dormant; growth is demand-side but not production-constrained.

> **IDP: Contango to Backwardation Shift**
> Gold futures remain in normal contango (forward prices above spot), reflecting carry cost financing. No movement toward backwardation has occurred. IDP is dormant. Futures market is not signaling physical scarcity. Prices are not inversion-levered.

## F. Investigation Tracks

### Physical Market Channel Checks

Dealer bid-ask spreads on gold kilobars (1kg wholesale units) are currently 1–3 cents/oz, indicating market liquidity and dealer inventory ease. Storage costs at major vaults (Brinks, G4S, Malca-Amit) are 1.25–1.50 basis points annually, unchanged from 2024 levels. No scarcity premium is evident in physical market pricing. Refineries report normal processing backlogs (6–8 weeks), with no shortage of semi-processed material or scrap inflows.

**Verification needed:** Contact major bullion dealers (Kitco, Apmex, JMBullion, BullionVault) to confirm bid-ask spread ranges and confirm inventory availability on key wholesale products. If spreads widen beyond 5–10 cents/oz or refinery backlogs exceed 16 weeks, physical market tightness would accelerate.

### Industrial Demand Verification

Semiconductor fabrication gold demand grew only +2–5% in 2025 despite record semiconductor capex spending ($150bn+). This suggests gold content per wafer is declining as manufacturers optimize interconnect layer formulations. Advanced device node roadmaps (Intel 18A, TSMC N2/N1.5, Samsung 3GAE) do not show increased gold requirements; instead, alternative metallurgy (copper-heavy interconnect, tungsten barrier layers) is reducing gold intensity.

**Verification needed:** Conduct primary research interviews with TSMC, Samsung, Intel, and equipment suppliers (Applied Materials, Lam Research, ASML) to confirm gold content trends in next-generation node designs. If gold content per wafer is declining, fabrication demand should be modeled at 200–250 tonnes by 2030 (flat to -5% CAGR), not growth-oriented.

### Macro Regime Positioning

The Federal Reserve's January 2026 pause in rate cuts, combined with forward guidance suggesting only 2–3 cuts in 2026 if recession materializes, has shifted market expectations. Gold's rally from $2,000 to $5,036 has outpaced nominal rate changes; the move is driven by real rate compression expectations (not realized) and geopolitical risk premium (realized).

If Fed policy shifts toward surprises tightening (e.g., February 2026 hold, no 2026 cuts), or if geopolitical tensions ease (BRICS coordination falters, Ukraine conflict resolved), gold could face a sharp correction to $4,200–$4,400/oz. Conversely, if recession data accelerates (unemployment spike, ISM manufacturing <45, yield curve re-inversion), gold could re-test $5,500–$5,700/oz.

**Verification needed:** Monitor weekly jobless claims data, ISM manufacturing PMI, credit spreads, and Fed speaker positioning. A confluence of recession signals + policy easing would confirm bull thesis; if growth remains resilient and Fed stays hawkish, bear scenario ($4,200–$4,400) becomes increasingly probable.

## G. Bull & Bear Cases

### Bull Case (60% Probability Assignment)

**Structural de-dollarization:** BRICS+ coalition expansion and gold-anchored settlement unit operationalization create 30–50 year tailwind for gold pricing. If BRICS captures 5–10% of global bilateral trade settlement (estimated $750bn–$1.5tn annually) by 2028, gold reserves held by BRICS central banks would increase from 6,000t to 9,000–12,000t, driving average prices to $5,500–$6,500/oz.

**Central bank acceleration:** 863t purchases in 2025 are at the upper bound of expectations; if 2026 delivers 900–1,100t purchases, gold supply deficit emerges (supply growth only +50–100t), forcing prices higher to equilibrate demand.

**Negative real rates in 2027–2028:** If recession materializes in late 2026, Fed cuts would accelerate to 100–150bps by mid-2027, driving 10Y TIPS real yields to -0.5% to +0.25%. Negative real rates would trigger institutional rebalancing, with gold rallying to $5,800–$6,200/oz.

**Geopolitical escalation:** Widening US-China tensions, Taiwan contingency planning, or NATO-Russia escalation would trigger acute safe-haven demand; gold could rally 20–30% in weeks to $6,000–$6,500/oz.

**Targeting:** Bull case suggests $5,500–$6,200/oz by Q4 2026, with 2027 upside to $6,800–$7,200/oz if recession + de-dollarization confirm.

### Bear Case (35% Probability Assignment)

**Sticky real rates:** If Fed maintains 3.5–3.75% through 2026 and inflation remains 2.3–2.7%, 10Y TIPS real yields could drift to +2.00–2.25%. At positive real rates above 2%, gold faces structural headwinds; zero-yield bullion becomes uncompetitive against short-term treasuries. Bear case suggests prices revert to $3,800–$4,200/oz.

**Speculative capitulation:** Net long liquidation to 165,000 contracts (37-week low) suggests speculative froth has deflated; if any negative catalyst (Fed tightening, recession fears ease, geopolitical tensions ease) occurs, cascade selling could accelerate to $4,200–$4,000/oz.

**Dollar rebound:** If Treasury supply concerns ease (higher global interest rates attract foreign demand), or if geopolitical de-dollarization stalls (BRICS currency fails), dollar could rally to 99–102 DXY. Dollar strength would be a direct headwind; gold could underperform to $4,000–$4,500/oz.

**Targeting:** Bear case suggests $4,200–$4,500/oz by Q4 2026, with potential washout to $3,500–$3,800/oz if recession fears ease and Fed returns to tightening posture.

**Probability Weighting:** Bull 60% / Bear 35% / Flat 5% reflects structural de-dollarization momentum (+), sticky real rates (-), and geopolitical baseline tail risks (+).
